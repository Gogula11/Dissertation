# Abstract

This dissertation investigates a hybrid approach combining Genetic Algorithms (GA) and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to solve the Parallel Machine Scheduling Problem with asymmetric, Sequence-Dependent Setup Costs (PMSP-SDSC). The problem is motivated by real-world manufacturing domains such as textile dyeing, where transitioning between colour batches incurs costs that depend on the specific colour pair and are asymmetric — dark-to-light transitions are expensive, while light-to-dark transitions are cheap. This cost structure renders classical scheduling heuristics inadequate, as they treat all inter-job transitions as equivalent.

A Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) agent is trained as a hyper-heuristic to dynamically select the GA's mutation operator during execution, replacing the fixed mutation strategy of a standalone GA with an adaptive policy that responds to the population's convergence state. A custom Gymnasium environment wraps the GA execution loop, exposing a 4-dimensional observation space (normalised best fitness, mean fitness, population diversity, and stagnation count) and a 3-action discrete space (swap, inversion, and aggressive swap mutation). The agent receives rewards proportional to the relative improvement in best fitness at each decision step.

The hybrid approach is evaluated against Shortest Processing Time (SPT), Nearest-Neighbour Greedy, and a standalone GA across six instance configurations (10, 20, and 50 jobs on 2 and 3 machines) with 30 random seeds each, totalling 720 experimental runs. Results show that the hybrid significantly outperforms all baselines on large instances: it achieves 63% lower composite cost than standalone GA (p < 0.01) and 53% lower than SPT (p < 0.001) on the large two-machine configuration. On small instances (n = 10), GA and Hybrid produce equivalent results, confirming that the hyper-heuristic is most valuable in large search spaces. Action frequency analysis reveals that the PPO agent learns an interpretable policy: it applies conservative mutation during early generations and escalates to aggressive exploration when stagnation is detected.

These findings demonstrate that DRL-guided mutation selection is an effective strategy for improving GA performance on challenging scheduling problems, and that the hyper-heuristic framing — controlling the search strategy rather than the search itself — is a tractable and generalisable approach to learning-enhanced combinatorial optimisation.
