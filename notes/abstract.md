# Abstract

This dissertation investigates a hybrid approach combining Genetic Algorithms (GA) and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to solve the Parallel Machine Scheduling Problem with asymmetric, Sequence-Dependent Setup Costs (PMSP-SDSC). The problem is motivated by real-world manufacturing domains such as textile dyeing, where transitioning between colour batches incurs costs that depend on the specific colour pair and are asymmetric — dark-to-light transitions are expensive, while light-to-dark transitions are cheap. This cost structure renders classical scheduling heuristics inadequate, as they treat all inter-job transitions as equivalent.

A Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) agent is trained as a hyper-heuristic to dynamically select the GA's mutation operator during execution, replacing the fixed mutation strategy of a standalone GA with an adaptive policy that responds to the population's convergence state. A custom Gymnasium environment wraps the GA execution loop, exposing an 8-dimensional observation space (normalised best fitness, mean fitness, population diversity, stagnation count, job count, machine count, mean setup cost, and mean colour darkness) and a 3-action discrete space (swap, inversion, and insertion mutation). The agent receives rewards proportional to the relative improvement in best fitness at each decision step. The environment is implemented using two evaluation profiles — a categorical baseline model with 7 colour classes and a continuous realistic model with 12 colour families, chemistry compatibility penalties, and customer priority segments — to assess the robustness of the hybrid approach under varying problem complexity.

The hybrid approach is evaluated against Shortest Processing Time (SPT), Nearest-Neighbour Greedy, and a standalone GA across six instance configurations (10, 20, and 50 jobs on 2 and 3 machines) with 50 random seeds each. Results show that the hybrid significantly outperforms the standalone GA on large instances under both profiles: it achieves 25-32% lower composite cost (p < 0.001) on the baseline profile and 14-24% lower composite cost (p < 0.001) on the realistic profile. On small and medium instances, GA and Hybrid produce equivalent results, confirming that the hyper-heuristic is most valuable in large search spaces where adaptive mutation control prevents premature convergence. Action frequency analysis reveals that the PPO agent learns an interpretable policy: it applies conservative swap mutation during early generations and escalates to exploration-oriented insertion mutation when stagnation is detected.

These findings demonstrate that DRL-guided mutation selection is an effective strategy for improving GA performance on challenging scheduling problems, and that the hyper-heuristic framing — controlling the search strategy rather than the search itself — is a tractable and generalisable approach to learning-enhanced combinatorial optimisation. The robustness of the hybrid advantage across two distinct instance profiles strengthens the case for its practical applicability.
