# Literature Reading Checklist

## Safe to cite without reading (well-known canon)

| Citation | Reason |
|----------|--------|
| Garey & Johnson (1979) | PMSP NP-hardness is textbook knowledge. Every scheduling paper cites this. |
| Pinedo (2016) | Standard textbook. Cited for definitions, not novel claims. |
| Holland (1975) | Origin of GAs — cited for historical context. |
| Wilcoxon (1945) | Statistical test paper. Standard library implementation used. |
| Nawaz, Enscore, Ham (1983) | NEH heuristic is well-documented. Used as a reference, not implemented. |

## Should read before submitting

| Citation | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Schulman et al. (2017) — PPO | Verify clipped surrogate objective description. Examiners may ask about PPO mechanics. |
| Burke et al. (2013) — Hyper-heuristics | Entire framing hinges on "hyper-heuristic" definition. Read section 2 to ensure accuracy. |
| Fortin et al. (2012) — DEAP | Uses DEAP's HallOfFame and toolbox architecture. Quick skim of relevant sections. |
| Raffin et al. (2021) — SB3 | Same as DEAP — quick skim. |

## Optional but strengthens the dissertation

| Citation | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Zhang et al. (2020) | DRL + scheduling. Reading positions the work relative to theirs. |
| Kool et al. (2019) | Attention models for routing. Establishes "DRL for combinatorial optimisation" narrative. |
| Thierens (2002) | Adaptive mutation in GAs. Directly relevant to the motivation. |
